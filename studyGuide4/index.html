Social Media Melting Pot
User Goals
When interacting with social media, users are likely to be simultaneously pursuing multiple of the following goals/reasons. Users are often interacting with social media to have social interaction, seek information, share information, be entertained, conduct communication, keep track of people, to be relaxed, and/or to waste time.
Platform Goals
Provide users tools to reach some or all of their goals. Promote general interaction. Attempt to develop a wholesome and useful culture. Grab users' attention and use it to grow their user base. Leverage their user base to make money.
Government Goals
Lawmakers and agencies are endowed with the responsibility to protect the public safety while using social media sites. Governments also wish to monitor social media and prevent unlawful action from happening. The government also endeavors to give users tools to allow for safer conscious use of the platforms.
User Actualities
Social media can often subvert the users intentions and instead lead unintended negative consequences. Many users find that social media causes anxiety or addiction. Additionally, social media can often promote hateful interactions. Specific bad actors and social media platform decisions can lead to information silos and opinion echo chambers. These social media environments can cause weaponization or radicalization of users. Effects of this nature are especially adverse if the user was intending to use the platform to seek/share information as either accidental or malicious distortions of information may cause users to believe something that isn’t completely true.
Platform Actualities
Social media platforms are competing for an ever more valuable piece of a users’ time. Companies must develop increasingly effective tools, features, and algorithms to keep users hooked. When algorithms are directed to optimize efforts to keep users interacting with the platform at all costs, the algorithm can promote unhealthy, addictive structures. Effective algorithms can build user quantity, but neither user quantity nor user happiness makes money. Selling things makes money. Internet services can be extraordinarily expensive at scale and companies must generate profits. Companies are often pressured to charge users for services, sell advertisements, and/or sell user data. Engineers allege that social media platforms were tracking every single action of a user they could sell. This included requests from telecommunication companies to track users' locations at all times and understand all the locations they might visit. Such invasions of privacy are often even completely legal due to exceptionally complex terms of service that most users never read. Companies are incentivised to keep their activities secret, hiding algorithms, practices, and decision making so their technology isn’t stolen and their users are blissfully unaware of the real product. This of course makes researching, understanding, and regulating social media more difficult.
Government Actualities
The same intrusive data gathering that the government could protect users from, is also one of the best sources of data for catching criminals. The individuals that make up the government are often biased by interactions with social media companies. Lawmakers or agency members could be swayed by monetary contribution, favors, or might have come from or be offered a position at tech companies. In the U.S., concerns over first amendment rights are increasingly often being made due to interaction on or from social media. Section 230 protects platforms from information shared on their service and allows the companies to control how information is shared as they see fit. However, some platforms exploit the protection by intentionally or unintentionally allowing their algorithms to promote hateful or immoral content. Creating law for social media is difficult especially for traditionally less tech literate lawmakers who do not understand the mechanisms of social media. But, even if there was legislation passed to address some social media downsides, it is difficult to apply. The internet is global and sites are often accessible from any location either by default or with simple tools.
